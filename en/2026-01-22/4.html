Divergent Global Reactions to Trump's Proposed Peace Council

Trump Board of Peace Proposal 2026-01-22 • 52 articles in total

In brief
  • US President Trump proposed a $1 billion fee Peace Council including Middle Eastern countries and Israel to address conflicts like Gaza.
  • Russia cautiously considers membership and finances while Western nations reject the council over UN concerns.
  • The initiative faces criticism for US-centric control, inclusion of autocrats, and uses economic pressure and religious outreach.

US President Donald Trump has proposed a new international 'Peace Council' aimed at resolving conflicts, particularly in Gaza, with a controversial $1 billion fee for permanent membership. The initiative has attracted participation from several Middle Eastern and Muslim-majority countries, including Saudi Arabia, Egypt, and Israel, with notable figures like Netanyahu agreeing to join. Russia, under President Putin, has shown cautious engagement, considering financial contributions and membership, while some Western and Scandinavian countries such as Norway, Sweden, France, and Italy have declined participation, expressing concerns about the council's legitimacy and its challenge to the United Nations. The proposal has sparked debate over its feasibility, geopolitical implications, and potential to reshape global diplomatic institutions, with critics wary of its US-centric control and rivalry with the UN. The involvement of religious leaders like the Pope and the use of economic pressure tactics further highlight the complex and multifaceted international responses to this initiative.

Middle Eastern and Muslim-majority Countries Supporting the Council

Several Middle Eastern and Muslim-majority countries, including Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Qatar, Turkey, and Morocco, have accepted invitations to join Trump's Peace Council. These nations view the initiative as a platform to foster regional cooperation and address conflicts such as the Gaza situation. Their participation signals a willingness to engage in new diplomatic frameworks despite some international skepticism.

Putin has expressed willingness to consider financial contributions, including allocating frozen assets, signaling a strategic interest in participating while maintaining diplomatic prudence. This stance reflects Russia's intent to influence international peace efforts without fully committing prematurely.

Israel's Participation Amidst Regional Tensions

Israel, represented by Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, has agreed to join the Peace Council despite ongoing military actions and tensions in Gaza. This participation is seen as an attempt to engage in diplomatic initiatives led by the US, potentially influencing peace negotiations. However, the juxtaposition of military escalations and diplomatic involvement raises questions about the council's effectiveness and Israel's strategic intentions.

Western and Scandinavian Countries' Rejection and Skepticism

Countries such as Norway, Sweden, France, and Italy have declined to join the Peace Council, expressing concerns about its legitimacy and the potential undermining of the United Nations. These nations emphasize adherence to established international institutions and caution against US-led alternatives perceived as unilateral or lacking transparency. Their rejection highlights divisions within the international community regarding new peace initiatives.

Critical Perspectives on the Council's Legitimacy and Impact

Several articles critique the Peace Council as a US-centric initiative that challenges the authority of the United Nations and raises doubts about its impartiality and effectiveness. Concerns include the high financial barrier for permanent membership, the inclusion of autocratic leaders, and the potential for escalating geopolitical tensions. This viewpoint underscores skepticism about the council's ability to deliver meaningful peace outcomes and its broader implications for global governance.

Involvement of Religious and Global Leaders and Diplomatic Maneuvers

The Peace Council has extended invitations to prominent figures such as the Pope, reflecting an attempt to incorporate moral and religious authority into the initiative. Additionally, reports of economic pressure tactics, like threats of tariffs to secure participation from countries like France, illustrate the unconventional and assertive diplomatic strategies employed. These elements highlight the complex and multifaceted nature of the council's formation and outreach efforts.
