EU's Strategic Phase-Out of Russian Gas by 2027 Amid Internal Divisions and Russian Criticism

EU Russia Energy Ban 2025-12-04 • 19 articles in total

In brief
  • The EU agreed to end Russian gas imports by 2027 to reduce dependency and enhance energy security.
  • Hungary and Slovakia oppose the ban due to their reliance on Russian energy, showing divisions within the EU.
  • Russia criticizes the ban, claiming it will weaken the EU's power and influence.

The European Union has reached a broad consensus to end imports of Russian gas by 2027, marking a pivotal shift in its energy policy aimed at reducing dependency on Russia amid the Ukraine conflict. This decision reflects a unified commitment among most member states to diversify energy sources and enhance energy security, despite logistical and economic challenges. However, internal dissent exists, notably from Hungary and Slovakia, which resist the ban due to their reliance on Russian energy supplies, highlighting fractures within the EU. Russia criticizes the move, warning it will weaken the EU's power and influence. Overall, the ban is a strategic effort to pressure Russia economically while accelerating the EU's transition to alternative and sustainable energy sources.

EU Majority Support for Phasing Out Russian Gas by 2027

Most EU member states and institutions emphasize the importance of ending Russian gas imports by 2027 to reduce dependency, increase energy security, and respond to geopolitical tensions related to the Ukraine war. They view this as a necessary strategic shift toward diversification and sustainability, despite the economic and logistical challenges involved. This perspective underscores a collective EU commitment to sanctions and resilience against Russian influence.

Internal EU Opposition and Concerns Over Energy Sovereignty

Certain EU members, particularly Hungary and Slovakia, oppose the ban on Russian gas imports due to their heavy reliance on Russian energy and concerns over energy sovereignty. Hungarian officials have publicly rejected the EU decision and filed appeals, reflecting fears about economic and energy security impacts. This dissent reveals significant divisions within the EU that could complicate unified implementation of the energy ban.

Russian Criticism of the EU Energy Ban

The Kremlin views the EU's decision to end Russian gas imports as detrimental to the EU's own power and influence, suggesting that the move will backfire on Europe. Russia frames the ban as a geopolitical miscalculation that will accelerate the EU's loss of energy strength and economic stability. This perspective highlights the ongoing economic and political tensions between Russia and the EU amid the Ukraine conflict.
