EU Divided on Using Frozen Russian Assets to Support Ukraine

EU Uses Frozen Russian Assets 2025-12-04 • 15 articles in total

In brief
  • The European Commission proposes using frozen Russian assets in the EU to fund loans and reparations for Ukraine's defense and reconstruction.
  • Most EU countries support this plan to aid Ukraine and pressure Russia, but Belgium opposes it over legal, financial, and sovereignty concerns.
  • This disagreement reveals EU challenges in balancing support for Ukraine with respecting national legal frameworks and member state consent.

The European Commission, led by Ursula von der Leyen, is actively proposing to use frozen Russian assets within the EU to provide substantial financial support to Ukraine amid the ongoing conflict. Proposals include large-scale loans and reparations funded by these assets, aiming to bolster Ukraine's military and reconstruction efforts. While many EU countries, including Germany, the Netherlands, Poland, Estonia, Slovakia, Italy, Ireland, Greece, and Albania, support these initiatives, Belgium has expressed significant legal, financial, and sovereignty concerns, opposing the confiscation without member state consent. This division highlights the challenges within the EU in balancing solidarity with Ukraine and respecting national interests and legal frameworks. The debate continues as the EU seeks consensus on leveraging these frozen assets effectively against Russia.

Supportive EU Member States Advocating Use of Frozen Assets

Countries such as Germany, the Netherlands, Poland, Estonia, Slovakia, Italy, Ireland, Greece, and Albania strongly support the European Commission's proposals to use frozen Russian assets to fund Ukraine. They emphasize the urgency of providing financial aid to Ukraine through loans and reparations, viewing these measures as critical to sustaining Ukraine's defense and reconstruction. These states align with the Commission's strategy to leverage economic pressure on Russia while assisting Ukraine's resilience.

Belgium's Opposition and Legal Concerns

Belgium stands out as a dissenting voice within the EU, opposing the use of frozen Russian assets without explicit consent from member states. Belgian political figures highlight the financial risks, legal challenges, and sovereignty issues involved in confiscating assets, stressing the need to respect private enterprise rights. This perspective reflects broader concerns about the legal and political implications of unilateral asset use within the EU framework, complicating consensus on the issue.
