Fragile truce and fraught diplomacy between Washington and Tehran

US–Iran Diplomacy 2026-04-10 • 136 articles in total

In brief
  • A two-week U.S.–Iran truce is mediated by Pakistan amid mutual distrust and conditional terms from both sides.
  • The U.S. demands compliance and warns of escalation while Iran insists on non-negotiable elements and sees gains.
  • The truce is fragile due to Israeli actions in Lebanon, international calls for restraint, and scepticism about lasting peace.

A flurry of last‑minute diplomacy has produced a conditional, short‑term pause in U.S.–Iran hostilities, with President Trump framing a two‑week suspension of attacks while warning Tehran that violations will bring consequences. Pakistan has emerged as the primary mediator and host for delegations, as Vice‑President J.D. Vance and other negotiators travel to Islamabad to seek a roadmap to a longer settlement. Tehran has responded with its own conditions—a published ten‑point plan and repeated statements that some issues (notably enrichment) are non‑negotiable—while both sides claim elements of victory even as mutual distrust endures. The truce is widely viewed as fragile: Israeli strikes in Lebanon, disputes over the Strait of Hormuz, and early alleged violations have prompted UN and international calls for restraint, market jitters, and sceptical assessments from analysts about the durability of any deal.

Washington frames the pause as leverage to secure a 'real' agreement and repeatedly emphasizes red lines and enforcement.
