EU Unites on €90 Billion Aid for Ukraine Amid Frozen Russian Assets Dispute

EU Ukraine Aid Drive 2025-12-19 • 49 articles in total

In brief
  • The EU agreed on a €90 billion financial aid package for Ukraine through loans and joint borrowing, avoiding use of frozen Russian assets.
  • Some EU countries oppose using frozen Russian assets, causing divisions and leading to contingency plans for financing.
  • Ukrainian President Zelensky urges rapid EU decisions to support Ukraine's defense and prevent further bloodshed.

The European Union has agreed on a substantial financial aid package of approximately €90 billion to support Ukraine through loans and joint borrowing mechanisms, reflecting a strong commitment to assist Ukraine amid ongoing conflict with Russia. However, the EU remains divided over the contentious issue of utilizing frozen Russian assets to fund this aid, with several member states, including Belgium and some Visegrad countries, opposing their use due to legal and political concerns. Ukrainian President Zelensky has actively urged EU leaders to finalize decisions swiftly, emphasizing the urgency of financial support to prevent further bloodshed. The EU's compromise to proceed with loans instead of direct asset seizure highlights both the bloc's unity in backing Ukraine and the complexities of internal negotiations. This financial strategy aims to sustain Ukraine's economy and war effort while navigating diplomatic sensitivities within the EU.

Countries covering this topic

Western and Core EU Supporters Favor Loan Package Without Russian Assets

Countries like Germany, France, Italy, and Sweden emphasize the importance of providing immediate financial aid to Ukraine through a €90 billion loan package, while avoiding the direct use of frozen Russian assets due to legal and diplomatic challenges. They view the loan agreement as a decisive message to Russia and a demonstration of EU unity and commitment to Ukraine's defense and reconstruction. Leaders such as Chancellor Merz and Ursula von der Leyen highlight the strategic and symbolic significance of this support, despite unresolved debates on asset use.

Eastern and Visegrad States Skeptical or Opposed to Using Russian Assets

Hungary, Slovakia, and the Czech Republic express reservations or outright refusal to participate in the €90 billion aid package, particularly opposing the use of frozen Russian assets. Leaders like Viktor Orbán label the financial aid as ineffective or 'lost money,' reflecting skepticism about the EU's approach. This group prioritizes caution regarding asset seizure and highlights internal EU divisions that complicate unified support for Ukraine.

Belgium and Some EU Members Resist Using Frozen Russian Assets

Belgium, along with political figures like Bart De Wever, strongly opposes releasing frozen Russian funds to Ukraine, citing risks and legal concerns. This resistance has led to deadlocks in EU negotiations and prompted contingency plans ('plan B') involving joint borrowing instead. The opposition underscores the complexity of balancing immediate financial aid with long-term legal and diplomatic ramifications within the EU.

Ukraine's Urgent Appeals and Participation in EU Negotiations

Ukrainian President Zelensky actively participates in EU summits and council talks, pressing for rapid decisions on financial aid and the use of frozen Russian assets. He frames the aid as critical to preventing further bloodshed and sustaining Ukraine's defense and reconstruction. Zelensky's involvement highlights Ukraine's reliance on EU support and the political pressure on member states to act decisively.
