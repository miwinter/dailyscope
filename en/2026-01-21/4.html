Divergent Global Reactions to Trump's Proposed Peace Council

Trump Board of Peace Proposal 2026-01-21 • 41 articles in total

In brief
  • Trump proposes a $1 billion fee Peace Council as an alternative to the UN for resolving global conflicts, especially Gaza.
  • UAE, Bahrain, Morocco, Belarus, and Russia's allies accept the invitation, showing interest in new peace efforts.
  • Many European countries reject the council, fearing it undermines the UN and objects to its high cost and legitimacy.

US President Donald Trump has proposed a new international 'Peace Council' aimed at resolving global conflicts, particularly focusing on the Gaza crisis. The initiative requires a $1 billion fee for permanent membership and is positioned as an alternative to the United Nations, drawing mixed reactions worldwide. Some countries, including the UAE, Bahrain, Morocco, Belarus, and Russia's allies, have accepted invitations to join, signaling regional and strategic interests. Conversely, many European nations and other global actors express skepticism or outright rejection, concerned about undermining the UN and the high financial demands. The proposal has sparked debate on the future of global governance, multilateralism, and the legitimacy of such a council in international diplomacy.

Countries covering this topic

Supportive Middle Eastern and Allied Nations

Countries like the United Arab Emirates, Bahrain, Morocco, and Belarus have accepted invitations to join Trump's Peace Council, viewing it as an opportunity to engage in new diplomatic efforts to resolve regional conflicts, especially in Gaza. Their acceptance reflects a willingness to explore alternative peace mechanisms and enhance their roles in international peace processes. These nations emphasize the council's potential to contribute to stability and peace in the Middle East and beyond.

Cautious or Skeptical European and Western Responses

Many European countries, including Germany, Norway, and the broader EU, have expressed caution or outright rejection of the Peace Council proposal, concerned it may undermine the United Nations and established international law frameworks. Officials worry about the high membership fee and the council's legitimacy, with some leaders like Annelena Baerbock openly opposing the initiative. This group reflects a preference for maintaining existing multilateral institutions and skepticism toward unilateral or financially driven peace efforts.

Major Powers and Strategic Invitations

Key global players such as Russia and China have been invited to join the Peace Council, with Russia's President Putin and his allies reportedly considering participation. China's invitation signals an interest in broader engagement, while Russia's involvement could influence Middle Eastern diplomacy. The US administration suggests Trump may continue to lead the council beyond his presidency, indicating a desire for sustained influence in global conflict resolution.

Controversies Over Financial and Political Aspects

The proposal's requirement of a $1 billion payment for permanent membership has drawn criticism and debate internationally, raising questions about the council's accessibility and motives. Trump's threats, such as imposing tariffs to coerce participation, and his claims that the council could replace the UN, have fueled skepticism. Critics view the initiative as a challenge to established diplomatic norms and question its feasibility and fairness.
