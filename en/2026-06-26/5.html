Hormuz tensions, supply shocks and a pivot to cleaner capacity

Global Energy Geopolitics 2026-06-26 • 54 articles in total

In brief
  • Iran asserts control over the Strait of Hormuz, enforcing navigation rules and causing security risks that disrupt shipping and spike oil prices.
  • The Russia-Ukraine conflict reduces Russian refining capacity and prompts Western maritime enforcement against Russian energy shipments.
  • Energy geopolitics evolve with producer output shifts, sanctions enforcement, and long-term renewables expansion shaping market and security dynamics.

Coverage is dominated by the Strait of Hormuz: Iranian authorities and the IRGC are asserting control over transit routes and warning ships that fail to comply, while repeated attacks and projectile strikes have triggered UN pauses in escort operations and raised insurance and security concerns. Those security risks produced short-term market volatility — oil prices spiked after reported strikes and fell again as traffic partially resumed — even as producer actions (Ras Tanura restarting, OPEC disputes) reshuffle supply expectations. Separately, the Russia–Ukraine conflict continues to damage Russian refining capacity and prompt domestic fuel shortages and panic-buying, with Western states tightening maritime enforcement against vessels linked to Russian energy shipments. At the same time, states and companies are advancing longer-term energy projects and decarbonisation efforts — from China’s rapid renewable rollout and direct renewable links for AI centres to hydro plant modernization and legal pressure on oil majors — underscoring that geopolitics and the energy transition are unfolding in tandem.

Iran asserts control over Hormuz transit

Tehran and its maritime authorities, including statements attributed to the IRGC and Hormuz authorities, insist that passage must follow Iran-defined routes and that ships acting outside those corridors bear the risks and insurance consequences. This posture frames Iran as asserting sovereign management of the chokepoint and seeks de facto leverage over navigation and security arrangements.

Attacks, paused escorts and market volatility

Multiple reports from regional and international outlets document attacks or strikes on vessels in and near the Hormuz Strait, the suspension of UN escort or evacuation initiatives, and a fragile rebound in ship traffic that remains below peacetime levels. Those security incidents have fed immediate market reactions — sudden oil price spikes after strikes and falls as shipments partially resume — and sustained concern about insurance, routing and supply-chain resilience.

Western enforcement and naval involvement

European states and international partners are emphasizing enforcement and maritime presence: France and other actors are detaining or inspecting tankers tied to alleged Russian evasion, and NATO/allied patrols and planned deployments by Western countries signal continued external policing of routes and sanctions. Russia–Ukraine strikes and domestic fuel pain in Russia

Reporting from varied sources highlights Ukrainian attacks on Russian oil infrastructure, including refinery shutdowns, and the resulting fuel shortages and panic-buying affecting Russian citizens. The coverage frames these strikes as materially degrading Russia's refining output and feeding domestic economic and political pressure.

Producer-state manoeuvres and OPEC politics

Major producers are recalibrating output and political posture: Saudi Aramco resumed loading at Ras Tanura, Iraq has publicly threatened to leave OPEC over quotas (even as Baghdad denies such moves), and smaller producers like Libya and exporters like Guyana are adjusting production and fiscal balances. These stories underscore how state-level decisions and intra-OPEC bargaining remain key determinants of near-term supply and revenue flows.

The cluster captures diplomatic friction over any attempt to monetize or re-route a globally vital chokepoint.
