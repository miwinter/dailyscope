Rethinking U.S. Conventional Strategy

US Military Strategy Reevaluation 2026-06-05 • 4 articles in total

In brief
  • U.S. military strategy is shifting to economic and policy tools like MADIS to counter cheap drone threats beyond kinetic methods.
  • Operational readiness issues and political constraints, such as damage to the USS Ford and canceled missile deployments, reveal vulnerabilities and restraint.
  • Experts warn these trends raise the risk of failing to secure a decisive conventional victory, urging strategic reassessment.

Recent reporting underscores a broad reassessment of U.S. conventional military strategy amid shifting threats and political constraints. Technical and policy innovations like the MADIS economic approach aim to blunt inexpensive drone proliferation and reflect thinking beyond pure kinetic solutions. Simultaneously, operational readiness concerns—highlighted by leaked footage of damage aboard the USS Ford—and political limits on deployments, such as canceling Tomahawk missiles to Germany to avoid Russian escalation, reveal vulnerabilities and restraint. Analysts warn these trends increase the risk that the United States could fail to secure a decisive conventional victory, arguing for urgent strategic recalibration.

Economic and non-kinetic countermeasures (MADIS)

This perspective emphasizes using economic tools and policy measures as strategic weapons to limit the spread and effectiveness of cheap drones. It frames MADIS as part of a broader shift toward non-kinetic and cost-imposing approaches to modern deterrence.

Operational readiness and transparency concerns (USS Ford)

This viewpoint focuses on immediate force readiness and the implications of equipment damage for naval capability and mission posture. Leaked footage of the USS Ford raises questions about maintenance, transparency, and the Navy's ability to sustain high-tempo operations.

  • Leaked video reveals fire damage aboard USS Ford

Strategic pessimism: risk of losing a conventional war

This analysis argues that changing warfare dynamics and doctrinal mismatches make a quick U.S. conventional victory increasingly uncertain. It calls for reappraising expectations, doctrine, and preparations for a potentially prolonged and costly conflict.

  • Toward the hundred days of war: the first conventional war the United States might not win since Korea

Political restraint and escalation management (Tomahawk cancellation)

This angle highlights how geopolitical and escalation concerns can constrain U.S. deployment decisions, as seen in plans to cancel Tomahawk missile placement in Germany. It suggests political limits and fear of retaliation are shaping force posture and deterrence choices in Europe.
