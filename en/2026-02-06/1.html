US-Russia Nuclear and Military Dialogue: Treaty Expiration and Dialogue Resumption

US–Russia Nuclear and Military Dialogue 2026-02-06 • 91 articles in total

In brief
  • The New START treaty has expired with Russia no longer bound, raising nuclear arms race and strategic stability concerns.
  • The US and Russia agreed to resume military talks to manage tensions amid Ukraine conflict and treaty expiration.
  • International and Ukrainian peace efforts continue with diplomatic talks, prisoner exchanges, and calls for renewed arms control including other nuclear powers.

The expiration of the New START nuclear arms control treaty between the United States and Russia marks a significant turning point, raising global concerns about a potential new nuclear arms race and diminished strategic stability. While Russia has declared it is no longer bound by the treaty, both countries have agreed to resume high-level military dialogue to reduce risks of miscalculations and manage tensions, particularly in the context of the ongoing Ukraine conflict. US President Donald Trump has been a prominent voice advocating for a new, improved nuclear treaty rather than extending the existing one, emphasizing modernization and inclusion of other nuclear powers like China. International actors, including the United Nations and the Pope, have expressed grave concerns about the treaty's expiration and urged renewed diplomatic efforts to prevent escalation. Concurrently, peace talks involving Ukraine, Russia, and the US continue with cautious optimism, including prisoner exchanges and ongoing negotiations in Abu Dhabi and plans for further talks in the US.

Russian Perspective: Treaty Expiration and Readiness to Negotiate

Russia officially announced that it no longer considers itself bound by the New START treaty, signaling a shift in nuclear arms control dynamics and raising concerns about strategic stability. Despite this, Russian officials express readiness to continue dialogue and negotiations with the United States on nuclear limits and military communication, emphasizing the importance of maintaining channels to reduce risks. This stance reflects a pragmatic approach amid heightened tensions and ongoing conflict in Ukraine.

US Perspective: Resuming Dialogue and Calls for New Treaty

The United States has agreed to resume high-level military talks with Russia to manage tensions and prevent misunderstandings, especially following the treaty's expiration. President Donald Trump has been vocal in rejecting the extension of New START, instead advocating for a new, modernized nuclear arms control agreement that could include other nuclear powers such as China. US officials emphasize the importance of dialogue and arms control to maintain global security despite current geopolitical challenges.

International and Third-Party Views: Concerns and Mediation Efforts

International organizations and third-party countries express deep concern over the expiration of the New START treaty, warning of increased risks of nuclear proliferation and global instability. The United Nations Secretary-General and the Pope have called for urgent negotiations to prevent escalation. Meanwhile, peace talks involving Ukraine, Russia, and the United States continue in venues like Abu Dhabi, with prisoner exchanges and diplomatic efforts ongoing. European and other international actors are also engaging in discreet talks to foster dialogue and de-escalation.

Ukrainian Perspective: Ongoing Conflict and Peace Negotiations

Ukraine remains actively engaged in peace negotiations with Russia, supported by the United States, aiming to resolve the ongoing conflict. President Zelensky has announced plans for military restructuring post-war and highlighted the need for European assistance. Despite heavy casualties and ongoing fighting, diplomatic efforts including prisoner exchanges and talks in Abu Dhabi continue, reflecting cautious optimism for de-escalation. Ukraine also underscores the security threats posed by Russia and the complex interplay of international actors in the conflict.
