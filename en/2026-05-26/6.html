US requires green-card processing from abroad: administrative shift and human costs

US Immigration Policy 2026-05-26 • 16 articles in total

In brief
  • The U.S. now requires most green-card applicants to complete residency processing from their home countries, causing longer waits and logistical challenges.
  • Stricter enforcement has led to protests, migrant deaths, repatriations, and hardships for affected families and communities.
  • The policy sparks political backlash and fear among diasporas, prompting public protests and mobilization by community leaders.

News outlets across multiple countries report a U.S. policy shift requiring most green-card applicants to file or complete permanent-residency processing from their home countries, a move presented as an administrative tightening aimed at changing timelines and screening. Coverage highlights immediate concerns about access, longer waits, and logistical burdens for applicants, with exceptions sometimes noted for national-interest cases. Separate reporting focuses on the human consequences of enforcement: detention protests, repatriation flights, long application waits, and commemorations of border deaths underline personal and community harms. Political and diaspora-focused pieces show backlash, fear of enforcement at public events, and how the rule is being framed domestically and internationally by affected communities and leaders.
