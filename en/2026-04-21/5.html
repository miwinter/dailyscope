Hormuz shocks, sanctions and shifting supply ties

Global Energy Geopolitics 2026-04-21 • 50 articles in total

In brief
  • Tensions in the Strait of Hormuz and the Russia–Ukraine conflict disrupt energy markets and strategic infrastructure, causing price spikes and diplomatic friction.
  • Regional and global actors compete to diversify energy supplies and secure markets through new pipelines, exports, investments, and coordinated resilience measures.
  • Great powers intensify naval and resource diplomacy to control energy routes and resources, while domestic policies influence global energy dynamics.

Escalating tensions in and around the Strait of Hormuz — including threats to close the chokepoint, reported strikes and redirected shipping — have driven sharp oil and gas price spikes and disrupted global shipping patterns. At the same time, the Russia–Ukraine conflict and debates over sanctions and waivers have turned energy infrastructure into a strategic target and a locus of diplomatic friction. Regional and bilateral moves, from Azerbaijan’s growing exports to new pipeline and power projects and African bids for LNG and mining investment, reflect competition to secure markets and diversify supplies. Great‑power posturing — naval transits, strategic reserve build‑outs and seabed/minerals diplomacy — is intensifying a scramble over routes and critical resources, prompting emergency stockpile proposals and calls for coordinated resilience measures.
