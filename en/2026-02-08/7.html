Trump's Peace Council Launches First Meeting Amid Mixed Global Reactions

Trump Peace Council Launch 2026-02-08 • 12 articles in total

In brief
  • US President Trump launched the Peace Council on February 19, 2026, to address the Gaza conflict and promote global peace efforts.
  • The council includes leaders like Hungary's Viktor Orbán and aims to mobilize international cooperation and a $1 billion Gaza reconstruction fund.
  • Some countries and media support the council's role in peace negotiations, while others, including Indonesia and Italy, express skepticism or refuse to participate due to concerns over its goals and impact on the UN.

US President Donald Trump has initiated the Peace Council, convening its first meeting on February 19, 2026, in Washington, focusing notably on the Gaza conflict and broader global peace efforts. The council aims to mobilize international cooperation, including a $1 billion fund for Gaza reconstruction, and involves influential global leaders such as Hungary's Viktor Orbán. While some countries and media highlight the council's potential to advance peace negotiations, others express skepticism or outright refusal to participate, citing concerns over its objectives and possible undermining of established international bodies like the United Nations. Indonesia and Italy have voiced reservations, with Indonesia threatening withdrawal if Palestinian goals are unmet. This initiative reflects both the ambition and the diplomatic complexities surrounding new US-led peace efforts in a tense geopolitical landscape.

US and Supportive International Media Perspective

Articles from US and allied media emphasize the launch of the Peace Council as a significant diplomatic initiative aimed at resolving global conflicts, particularly focusing on Gaza. They highlight the planned meeting date, the involvement of key leaders, and the council's goals to foster peace and mobilize financial support for reconstruction efforts. This viewpoint presents the council as a proactive and strategic effort to mediate ongoing disputes and promote international cooperation.

European and Other Countries' Skepticism and Reservations

Several European countries and others express skepticism or decline participation in the Peace Council, citing concerns about its framework, objectives, and potential to undermine existing international institutions like the UN. Italy's explicit refusal and Greece's cautionary stance reflect diplomatic complexities and doubts about the council's legitimacy and effectiveness. This perspective underscores the challenges faced by the US-led initiative in securing broad multilateral support.

Mixed International Engagement and Conditional Support

Some countries show conditional engagement with the Peace Council, balancing participation with political considerations. Hungary's Prime Minister Viktor Orbán's confirmed attendance contrasts with Indonesia's warning to withdraw if Palestinian objectives are not met, illustrating the nuanced and sometimes fragile nature of international cooperation within the council. This viewpoint highlights the geopolitical sensitivities influencing countries' involvement in the initiative.
