China and Russia's Expanding Autocratic Influence and Global Democratic Challenges

China-Russia Autocracy Push 2026-03-18 • 10 articles in total

In brief
  • China and Russia lead a coordinated global authoritarian network challenging democracies and international stability.
  • These regimes exploit democratic weaknesses and Western aid retrenchment to expand influence, especially in Europe and the Global South.
  • Some view China’s political model as an alternative form of democracy, suggesting ideological competition beyond confrontation.

Multiple analyses highlight China and Russia as central architects of a growing global authoritarian network that challenges democratic norms and international stability. These regimes are seen as coordinating across borders with allied autocracies, employing tactics such as influence operations, political pressure, and infrastructure collaboration to extend their reach, including within Europe and the Global South. Western democracies are criticized for underestimating this coordinated autocratic push, which exploits democratic weaknesses and retrenchment in foreign aid. Meanwhile, some perspectives present China's governance model as an alternative form of people's democracy, suggesting ideological competition beyond mere confrontation. The geopolitical implications include heightened security concerns, shifts in alliance dynamics, and calls for more strategic and adaptive democratic responses.

Western and European Security Perspectives on Autocratic Threats

This viewpoint, represented by articles from Sweden, Belgium, Austria, Estonia, and Moldova, emphasizes the security risks posed by China and Russia's coordinated efforts to undermine democratic institutions and influence political systems globally, including within Europe. It highlights concerns about election interference, suppression of opposition, and the strategic consolidation of autocratic networks that threaten liberal democracies and international order. The perspective calls for increased vigilance and policy responses to counter these challenges.

Articles from Portugal, Italy, and Slovenia focus on the structural and methodological aspects of the expanding authoritarian bloc led by China and Russia. They analyze how these regimes institutionalize cooperation with allied autocracies and exploit Western retrenchment in foreign aid to strengthen their influence, particularly in the Global South. This viewpoint stresses the weakening unity among democracies and the need for strategic, adaptable policies to confront the growing autocratic international network.
