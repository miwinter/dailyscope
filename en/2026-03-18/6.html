EU-Ukraine Cooperation to Restore Druzhba Pipeline Amid Divergent Regional Views

EU Energy Diplomacy 2026-03-18 • 11 articles in total

In brief
  • The EU supports restoring the Druzhba pipeline with Ukraine's financial and technical help to ensure regional energy stability.
  • Hungary links energy supply restoration to Ukraine's financial aid, using energy as leverage in political negotiations.
  • Ukraine accuses some European partners of political blackmail over the use of energy infrastructure.

The European Union is actively supporting the restoration of the Druzhba oil pipeline to stabilize energy supplies in Central and Eastern Europe, with Ukraine accepting financial and technical assistance. This cooperation underscores the EU's commitment to regional energy security despite ongoing geopolitical tensions and strikes affecting pipeline operations. However, divergent perspectives emerge within the region: Hungary links energy flows to Ukraine's financial aid, while Ukraine accuses some European actors of political coercion related to pipeline politics. EU leaders face internal disagreements on broader energy policies, reflecting the complexity of balancing energy security, financial support, and political unity. The EU also explores parallel initiatives to secure global energy routes, such as the Strait of Hormuz, highlighting its comprehensive energy diplomacy efforts.

Hungary's Conditional Stance Linking Energy to Financial Aid

Hungary, represented by Prime Minister Viktor Orban, views the restoration of oil supplies via the Druzhba pipeline as a prerequisite for Ukraine to receive financial support. This perspective ties energy resource availability directly to economic aid, reflecting Hungary's strategic use of energy dependence in broader political and financial negotiations within the EU framework.

Ukraine's Accusation of Political Coercion in Energy Diplomacy

From Ukraine's viewpoint, some European partners are accused of leveraging energy infrastructure, particularly the Russian pipeline to Hungary, as a form of political blackmail. This stance highlights tensions and mistrust within European energy diplomacy, underscoring the contentious nature of energy as a tool in geopolitical conflicts and intra-EU relations.
