EU Unites on Large Loan for Ukraine, Rejects Using Frozen Russian Assets

EU Ukraine Aid Drive 2025-12-20 • 60 articles in total

In brief
  • The EU agreed on a €90 billion aid package for Ukraine financed by joint borrowing and eurobonds, rejecting frozen Russian assets over legal and political concerns.
  • Several EU members, including Belgium, Hungary, Slovakia, and the Czech Republic, opposed using Russian assets and the loan plan due to differing national interests.
  • Germany and France face domestic political challenges balancing Ukraine support with fiscal and political pressures.

The European Union has agreed on a substantial financial aid package, approximately €90 billion ($105-$182 billion depending on source), to support Ukraine's economy and war efforts over the next two years. Despite initial proposals, EU leaders decided against using frozen Russian assets to finance this aid due to legal, political, and diplomatic concerns, opting instead for joint borrowing and eurobond issuance. This compromise reflects divisions within the EU, with countries like Belgium, Malta, Hungary, Slovakia, and the Czech Republic expressing reservations or dissent. Ukrainian President Zelensky and supportive EU members emphasize the urgency and importance of this aid, while some political figures in Germany and France express concerns about the financial and political costs. Overall, the EU demonstrates strong commitment to Ukraine's defense and reconstruction amid complex internal and external challenges.

Supportive EU Members Advocating Strong Financial Aid

Countries such as Poland, Belgium, and leaders like Donald Tusk and Ursula von der Leyen emphasize the necessity of substantial financial support to Ukraine, viewing it as a shared European interest and a fight for democracy and security. They back the €90 billion loan package and stress unity despite internal disagreements. Their perspective highlights solidarity with Ukraine and the importance of sustained aid without escalating tensions by seizing Russian assets.

Countries Opposing Use of Frozen Russian Assets and Expressing Caution

Belgium, Malta, and some other EU members challenge the plan to use frozen Russian assets for Ukraine aid, citing legal, procedural, and political concerns. Hungary, Slovakia, and the Czech Republic notably dissented from the joint loan agreement, reflecting divergent national interests and caution about financial commitments. This group favors alternative financing methods like joint borrowing and eurobonds to avoid legal complications and potential retaliation from Russia.

Political and Economic Concerns Within Key EU States

Germany and France face internal political challenges regarding the scale and method of financial support to Ukraine. German Chancellor Friedrich Merz experienced political setbacks despite negotiating the aid package, while French President Macron faces criticism over fiscal sustainability. These concerns reflect the balancing act between supporting Ukraine and managing domestic political and economic pressures, highlighting the complexity of EU consensus-building.
