Hormuz shocks, sanctions waivers and a global rush for energy security

Global Energy Geopolitics 2026-04-18 • 83 articles in total

In brief
  • The Strait of Hormuz is a key source of oil-price volatility amid regional tensions and evolving sanctions on Russian oil.
  • Asian countries accelerate nuclear and renewable energy to reduce fossil fuel dependence, while Europe faces acute fuel vulnerabilities and inflation risks.
  • Global energy infrastructure is fragile, with states seeking financial cushions amid attacks, disruptions, and rising transport costs.

News across regions show the reopening and contestation of the Strait of Hormuz driving sharp oil-price swings and market volatility, with traders and investors rapidly repricing supply risk. At the same time the United States and allies have repeatedly extended temporary waivers or permissions for Russian oil shipments to dampen shortages, a pragmatic step that reshapes sanctions dynamics. Asian actors and China are accelerating nuclear and low‑carbon capacity to reduce reliance on volatile fossil fuel markets, while Europe faces acute fuel vulnerabilities flagged by the IEA and central bankers. Developing and island states are seeking financial cushions, stockpiles and contingency shipments as transport and aviation costs rise, and attacks, spills and pipeline incidents underline the fragility of energy infrastructure worldwide.
