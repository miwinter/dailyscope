US Expands Social Media Screening for Visa and Visa-Exempt Travelers

US Immigration: Social Media Screening 2025-12-11 • 15 articles in total

In brief
  • The US requires foreign travelers from 40-42 countries, including skilled workers and tourists, to provide five years of social media history for visa and entry screening.
  • These policies aim to enhance national security but raise global privacy and civil liberty concerns.
  • The measures apply to various visa categories and affect a wide range of countries, balancing security with individual rights.

The United States is implementing and proposing new policies requiring foreign travelers, including skilled workers and tourists from about 40-42 countries, to disclose up to five years of social media history as part of visa and entry screening processes. These measures aim to enhance national security by enabling more thorough background checks and threat assessments. The policies affect various visa categories such as H-1B, H-4, and visa waiver program travelers, expanding scrutiny beyond traditional visa applicants. While the government emphasizes security benefits, these proposals have raised significant privacy and civil liberty concerns internationally. The debate highlights tensions between border security priorities and individual rights across multiple countries.

Countries covering this topic

Indian Skilled Worker Visa Screening

India-focused articles highlight the introduction of social media vetting specifically for H-1B and H-4 visa applicants, who are skilled workers and their families. This reflects a targeted approach to enhance security checks within a key immigrant worker group, emphasizing the US government's intent to tighten immigration controls on high-skilled labor entrants.

Visa-Exempt and Tourist Travelers Face Expanded Screening

Articles from South Korea, Costa Rica, Cyprus, Moldova, Chile, North Macedonia, Belgium, Finland, Montenegro, Poland, Luxembourg, Hong Kong, Bolivia, and the EU/GB region discuss the broadening of social media screening to tourists and visa-exempt travelers. These sources focus on the requirement to submit five years of social media history, highlighting concerns about privacy, potential discrimination, and the impact on international tourism. The viewpoint underscores the policy's wide geographic reach and the balance between security and civil liberties.
