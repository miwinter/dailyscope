U.S. shifts green-card processing abroad

US Immigration Policy 2026-05-23 • 15 articles in total

In brief
  • The U.S. now requires most green-card applicants to leave the country and apply for permanent residency via consulates in their home countries.
  • Lawyers warn this policy risks family separations, disrupts migration pathways, and may trigger legal challenges due to increased backlogs.
  • A U.S. court ruling limits aggressive deportations amid ongoing judicial scrutiny of enforcement policies.

Multiple international outlets report a U.S. policy change requiring most green-card applicants to leave the United States and pursue permanent-residence requests through consular processing in their home countries. Coverage highlights procedural consequences for applicants, sponsors and employers, with officials citing security and standardization while analysts warn of increased backlogs and verification steps. Immigration lawyers and advocates caution the rule could separate families, disrupt established adjustment-of-status routes and prompt litigation, and a U.S. court ruling in Tennessee underscores ongoing judicial challenges to aggressive enforcement measures. Separate pieces document operational outcomes—expulsions and repatriations in the region—and practical travel information about visa-waiver arrangements that remain relevant for short-term visitors.
